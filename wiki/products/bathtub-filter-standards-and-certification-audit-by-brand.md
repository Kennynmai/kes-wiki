# Bathtub Filter Standards and Certification Audit by Brand

| Brand | Surfaced standard/certification wording | Quote anchor | Preliminary reading | Audit priority |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sprite | explicit NSF/ANSI 177 wording visible on shower cartridge pages; narrower chlorine story | “The Sprite Twist Off replacement cartridge… is certified to NSF/ANSI Standard 177” | strongest surfaced standard-adjacent trust posture in this set, but mostly from shower lineage | medium-high |
| Canopy | surfaced “tested to” / not-certified ambiguity in reviewer coverage | WaterFilterGuru: “tested to NSF 177 Standards… not the same as an official certification” | important wording-risk case study | high |
| Santevia | not strong on surfaced certification language in this pass | more product/support cadence and comfort framing than visible standard-badge emphasis | lower surfaced certification emphasis | medium |
| Crystal Quest | broad contaminant claims surfaced; certification not strongly surfaced in this pass | broad removal claims visible without equally strong surfaced certification anchors | broad-claim / proof mismatch risk | high |
| Kinder | clinical language stronger than standard/certification language in surfaced results | “clinically tested to improve skin hydration” | high need to inspect what “clinically tested” actually means | very high |
| Tubo | “CLINICALLY TESTED” surfaced strongly; no strong certification anchors surfaced in this pass | “CLINICALLY TESTED” on product page | high need to inspect test basis and scope | very high |

## Working conclusion
The most important standards/certification problem in this category may not be the absence of standards. It may be the **blending of certification-style authority, testing language, and clinical/improvement language into one marketing impression**.
