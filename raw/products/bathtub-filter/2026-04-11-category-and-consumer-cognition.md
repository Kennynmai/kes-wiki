# Raw Capture — Bathtub Filter Category and Consumer Cognition (2026-04-11)

## Purpose
First-layer capture focused on category definition, category boundary, and target-consumer mental models.

## Core category-definition signals observed
### Bathtub filter is framed as an attachment, not as a plumbing system
Repeated public descriptions imply:
- easy-to-install attachment
- hangs over tub faucet / tub spout
- fits most tubs / fits most faucets
- portable / apartment-friendly / rental-friendly

This suggests consumer cognition is closer to “smart add-on bath accessory” than “serious water-treatment system.”

### Bathtub filter is sold as a bathing-experience improver
Repeated outcome language:
- gentler bathwater
- cleaner-feeling bathwater
- softer skin / smoother hair
- reduced chlorine smell
- more pleasant soaking / bathing

This suggests the category competes on perceived comfort and irritation reduction more than on strict engineering literacy.

## Consumer mental-model signals observed
### Segment 1: parents of babies / young children
Repeated patterns:
- baby bath filter
- delicate skin
- temperature sensor / bump protection / baby-safe framing
- pediatrician / mom framing in DTC storytelling

Likely consumer mindset:
- risk reduction for a vulnerable user
- willing to pay for gentler or safer-feeling bathing
- wants easy install and low-friction daily use

### Segment 2: sensitive-skin / eczema households
Repeated patterns:
- sensitive skin
- eczema-prone skin
- dry / itchy / irritated skin
- “may help reduce irritation or dryness” style language

Likely consumer mindset:
- searching for relief from discomfort
- may be highly motivated but also disappointment-prone
- may react strongly to trust cues and reviews

### Segment 3: chlorine / odor / harsh-water avoiders
Repeated patterns:
- chlorine smell
- harsh chemicals
- gentler water
- reduced odor

Likely consumer mindset:
- wants noticeable sensory improvement
- may not need medical-style claim language
- likely values immediate, noticeable difference

### Segment 4: apartment / rental workaround buyers
Observed from public forum-style snippets:
- cannot install whole-house filtration
- may use shower filter or bath-ball filter as workaround
- wants something non-permanent and movable

Likely consumer mindset:
- practicality first
- not seeking perfect filtration, just “better than nothing” or “good enough improvement”

## Category-boundary signals observed
### Bathtub filter vs shower filter
Public discussion often treats bathtub filter as adjacent to shower filter, not fully separate from it.
Observed logic:
- shower filter is better known
- bathtub filter is often recommended when the user specifically soaks, bathes children, or fills a tub
- some consumers use shower filters with handheld hose setups to fill the tub instead of buying a dedicated bath filter

Implication:
- bathtub filter may be a subcategory or derivative demand branch rather than a fully independent core category

### Bathtub filter vs bath ball filter
Public language sometimes uses these almost interchangeably.
Implication:
- “bath ball” may be one of the strongest consumer-facing sub-forms inside the broader bathtub-filter category
- terminology is not yet cleanly standardized

### Bathtub filter vs whole-house / under-sink style filtration
Editorial and discussion-style sources repeatedly imply:
- whole-house filtration is the more complete solution
- bath filters are easier, cheaper, and more accessible workarounds

Implication:
- category may win as a convenience/compromise product, not as the ultimate solution

## Consumer benefit signals that seem most intuitive
Most intuitive / believable benefits in public cognition:
- less chlorine smell
- gentler-feeling water
- softer post-bath skin feel
- easier solution for babies and rentals

Less stable / more controversial benefits:
- fluoride removal
- true hard-water softening
- broad contaminant elimination
- disease-style skin outcome claims

## Friction / skepticism signals observed
- hard to hang properly
- takes time to dry
- uncertainty about whether it really works
- concern about whether all water truly passes through filter media
- practical compromise positioning (“good alternative,” “best option I found for apartments”) rather than miracle-solution language in user discussion

## First-layer interpretation
Current public cognition suggests bathtub filter is understood less as a filtration-technology purchase and more as:
- a baby/sensitive-skin support accessory
- a chlorine-reduction comfort product
- a renter/apartment workaround
- a lower-commitment alternative to bigger filtration systems
