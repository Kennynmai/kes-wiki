## What this source is for
This source is for first-layer understanding of:
- what a bathtub filter is in consumer language
- where the category boundary sits
- who appears to buy it and why

## Key extracted facts
### Category cognition
- Bathtub filter is generally described as an easy attachment or hang-on product, not a serious plumbing installation.
- The category is sold around gentler bathing, chlorine reduction, and skin comfort.

### Target-consumer cognition
Strongest repeated user groups:
- parents buying for babies / children
- sensitive-skin / eczema households
- chlorine-conscious users who notice odor or harshness
- renters / apartment users looking for easy workarounds

### Category boundary
- Bathtub filter is adjacent to shower filter and sometimes treated as a sub-branch of that demand.
- “Bath ball filter” appears to function as both a product form and a consumer-facing term within the category.
- Whole-house filtration is often presented as the more complete option, with bath filters positioned as lower-friction compromise solutions.

### Most intuitive benefits in public cognition
- less chlorine smell
- gentler-feeling water
- softer skin feel after bathing
- easier solution for babies / renters / temporary setups

### More fragile or controversial benefits
- fluoride removal
- broad contaminant-removal promises
- true hard-water softening
- strong medical-style skin outcome claims

## What this source suggests
The category may be more accurately understood as a problem-solution accessory category than as a standard bathroom hardware category.

That means success likely depends on:
- emotional and trust framing
- easy-install credibility
- believable sensory outcomes
- clear “good enough workaround” logic for non-whole-house buyers
