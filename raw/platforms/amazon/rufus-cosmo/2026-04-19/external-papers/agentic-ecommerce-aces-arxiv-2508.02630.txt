What Is Your AI Agent Buying?
Evaluation, Biases, Model Dependence, & Emerging
Implications for Agentic E-Commerce

Abstract
Online marketplaces will be transformed by autonomous AI agents acting on behalf of consumers. Rather than humans browsing and clicking, AI agents can parse webpages or leverage
APIs to view, evaluate and choose products. We investigate the behavior of AI agents using ACES, a provider-agnostic framework for auditing agent decision-making. We reveal that
agents can exhibit choice homogeneity, often concentrating demand on a few “modal” products while ignoring others entirely. Yet, these preferences are unstable: model updates can
drastically reshuffle market shares. Furthermore, randomized trials show that while agents
have improved over time on simple tasks with a clearly identified best choice, they exhibit
strong position biases—varying across providers and model versions, and persisting even in
text-only ”headless” interfaces—undermining any universal notion of a “top” rank. Agents
also consistently penalize sponsored tags while rewarding platform endorsements, and sensitivities to price, ratings, and reviews vary sharply across models. Finally, we demonstrate that
sellers can respond: a seller-side agent making simple, query-conditional description tweaks
can drive significant gains in market share. These findings reveal that agentic markets are
volatile and fundamentally different from human-centric commerce, highlighting the need for
continuous auditing and raising questions for platform design, seller strategy and regulation.
Keywords: AI shopping agents, agentic e-commerce, platform design, marketplaces, sponsored ads, product ranking, recommendation systems, Vision Language Models

Introduction

In a significant structural change for e-commerce, AI tools for shopping assistance have recently
become available to consumers, and are quickly gaining adoption [13]. Industry leaders are already signaling strategic disruption; Walmart’s CTO urges preparedness for third-party agents
that circumvent traditional merchandising and advertising cues.1 In particular, autonomous AI
agents offer the capability to take over the shopping journey entirely including product selection.
A new generation of “computer-use” systems—such as Agent mode in OpenAI’s Atlas browser
or Google’s Project Mariner which can automate grocery orders, fill forms, and book flights—
promises to replace manual browsing with autonomous procurement. With such tools becoming
widely available, there is a need for a principled framework to study, evaluate and monitor these
systems.
AI tools have the potential to significantly reduce search frictions by systematically scanning
broader assortments than humanly possible. Yet, this raises a critical question: are AI agents
the perfectly rational optimizers that economic theory has long envisioned, or are they a new
species of decision-maker with their own unique, exploitable biases? If the latter, do these tools
dilute the influence of classical monetization levers, such as product rankings and sponsored ads?
Parallel questions arise on the seller side: is there scope for “AI-SEO” where sellers tune listings
to anticipate buyer-agent behavior? The emergence of these tools foreshadows a reconfigured
e-commerce ecosystem, raising novel design, strategy, and regulatory challenges. With this shift
upon us, we ask the fundamental question: what is our AI agent buying—and why? More concretely,
we investigate this through various layers of inquiry.
1. Rational Behavior. Do agents satisfy basic instruction following and simple economic dominance tests? And how are such capabilities evolving over time?
2. Product Market Shares. What market shares emerge when purchases are fully mediated by AI
agents, and how do they vary across model providers and generations?
3. Choice Behavior and Biases. How do AI agents respond to observable attributes (price, rating,
reviews, text) and platform levers (position, promotions, sponsorship)? And to what extent
are such responses stable across providers and model generations?
4. Interaction between Buyer and Seller Agents. How might outcomes change when sellers and/or
marketplace platforms deploy their own optimizing AI agents?
The first question concerns minimum viability: without elementary rationality, higher-order
design or policy levers may be moot (cf. the emphasis on baseline competence by Visa CEO in

To investigate the aforementioned research questions, we construct a controllable agent-platform
sandbox dubbed ACES and conduct randomized experiments that (i) test for instruction following
and basic rationality, (ii) reveal market shares, (iii) cleanly isolate and quantify the effects of listing
characteristics and platform tags on agent decision making, and (iv) understand the impact of a
strategic response by a seller who optimizes their product description. Our main contributions
and findings can be summarized as follows.
• ACES as an evaluation framework. We develop ACES (Agentic e-CommercE Simulator), a
provider- and protocol-agnostic sandbox that pairs (i) a multi-modal large language model
with (ii) a fully programmable mock e-commerce application (Figure 3). By randomizing
page layout, listing order, prices, ratings, reviews, and promotional badges, ACES provides a
realistic yet controlled setting to causally identify how platform levers and product attributes
shape agent choices. ACES is an evaluation framework inspired by behavioral economics/operations—not a leaderboard benchmark with objective labels. In label-free domains such
as e-commerce, it assesses agent behavior through synthetically randomized experiments
that recover causal choice responses. Our focal implementation uses a computer-use vision
language model (VLM) that parses retailer webpages, but we can (and do) apply the same
approach to headless/API interfaces where the platform exposes structured product data
(e.g., a ranked JSON list) instead of a visual webpage (Section 7.2). Thus, ACES offers practitioners a novel way to evaluate shopping agents independent of the interaction mode. As
shopping is increasingly delegated to AI and becomes predominantly digital, an ACES-style
sandbox can approximate the true operating environment—it can help evaluate and monitor
agent behavior, enable simulation-based optimization of platform design and seller strategy,
and anticipate market equilibria that will arise between buyer agents, seller optimizers, and
ranking algorithms.
In this paper, we use ACES to evaluate three focal model families at two snapshots in time:
Claude Sonnet 4, GPT-4.1, Gemini 2.5 Flash (the state of the art models as of August, 2025)
as well their latest successors (as of December 1, 2025), Claude Opus 4.5, GPT-5.1, Gemini
3.0 Pro Preview. To get clean experimental results and insights, we focus on buyer agent
behavior under one shot product selection (our setup is discussed in Section 2).
• Baseline rationality. We assess two primitives—instruction following and economic rationality—using randomized choice sets with exactly one correct option (the only item meeting
a stated constraint, or the unique dominator on price or rating). We observe a consistent

the six models, AI agents react positively to higher average ratings and to a larger number

• Robustness Analysis. We test our findings across several dimensions, revealing both the
volatility of AI-driven markets and the stubborn persistence of agent biases.
(i) Model finalization act as demand shocks. The post training that took place while transitioning from Gemini 2.5 Flash Preview to the official release substantially reallocated
market share across products and inverted the model’s position bias from the bottom
row to the top row (Section D).
(ii) Biases Resist Simple Prompting. Explicitly prompting an agent to “ignore position” does
not significantly mitigate position effects. Furthermore, while adding a mention that
the buyer is “price-sensitive” steepens the agent’s price sensitivity as intended, significant position effects remain (Section 7.3 & Appendix B.2.3).
(iii) Effects Persist in “Headless” Environments. We replicated our experiments on three focal models in a “headless” API setting—providing agents only with a ranked JSON
list of product data and no images—and once again found similar biases, e.g., strong,
model-specific position effects, that persist even under prompt variations (Section 7.2).
This suggests position and other effects are not driven (only) by visual processing from
parsing a webpage, and are likely to persist under standardized agent protocols.
While specific empirical coefficients—such as the position bias in Gemini 2.5 Flash versus 3.0
Pro Preview—capture a moment in technological time, the structural market vulnerabilities they
reveal are enduring. Our longitudinal analysis across model generations for multiple providers
exposes a critical distinction in the evolution of different aspects of AI model behavior: while
agents have improved over time to achieve near-perfect consistency in basic economic rationality
tasks, they remain fundamentally prone to strong model dependence of market shares, choice homogeneity, and highly model-dependent position biases. Crucially, the persistence of these effects
in “headless” (API-only) environments and their resistance to corrective prompting indicates that
they are not superficial artifacts of the interface used, but deep-seated characteristics of AI models. This suggests that without intervention, such distortions will remain a structural feature of
the agentic economy in the medium term.
Given the volatility in market shares and the persistence of biases across generations, our
work highlights the necessity for continuous monitoring and auditing of these AI shopping mod6

els and ACES provides such an auditing framework. We develop ACES (Agentic e-Commerce
Simulator) as a foundation for Algorithmic Economic Auditing—a necessary infrastructure for
market governance. Just as financial markets rely on standardized audits to detect irregularities,
AI-mediated markets require continuous, counterfactual auditing to quantify friction and detect
competitive distortions. ACES provides the first (to our knowledge) rigorous, provider-agnostic
framework for this purpose. By shifting the evaluative lens from engineering-centric “task completion” benchmarks to economic “choice behavior” auditing, this framework offers stakeholders
a governable methodology to assess competitive neutrality, seller visibility, and market integrity
in a marketplace where the primary decision-maker is a black box.
We note that our finding of biases which persist across many variations of our experiment
mirrors similar findings in other contexts, e.g., race and gender disparities in AI-based hiring
[13]. We also flag here our observation that biases are impacted significantly by the post training
process, as seen in the impact of Model finalization (Section D; summary above).

1.1

Related Literature

Our work connects to a rich body of literature spanning computer-use agents, e-commerce systems, recommendation and ranking algorithms, behavioral biases in human decision-making, and
recent advances in multi-modal AI agents. Across these threads, our contribution is to provide
a controlled, visual, and interactive sandbox that zooms in on the choice step (which product to
buy) for AI shopping agents, and measures rationality and choice behavior via randomized page
layouts, attributes, and badges. Although ACES resembles prior computer-use testbeds such as
WebArena and VisualWebArena, our emphasis is different: rather than evaluating end-to-end
web navigation, we focus on a single critical step—selecting which product to buy—to obtain a finegrained view of agent choice behavior in shopping contexts [26, 50]. While most benchmarks for
AI agents evaluate end-to-end task completion, our contribution is to isolate the critical choice
step in agent shopping through the controllable ACES sandbox. This facilitates the first agentcentric, causal evaluation of how AI shoppers respond to product attributes and platform levers
like product positions and promotional badges, complementing existing studies on human consumers [29, 41].
Computer-Use Agents and Benchmarks.

A fast-growing body of work builds web and OS-

interacting agents evaluated in realistic environments. WebArena introduces reproducible, longhorizon web tasks over functional sites (e.g., e-commerce, forums), with baseline agents achieving low end-to-end success relative to humans [50]. VisualWebArena extends to multimodal interfaces with images and forms [26]. Mind2Web targets generalist web agents across 137 real
websites [10], and follow-ups examine grounding for GPT-4V as a generalist web agent [49]. Beyond the browser, OSWorld provides a real-computer environment (Ubuntu, Windows, macOS)

7

and documents large headroom between SOTA agents and humans [44]; Windows Agent Arena
and AndroidWorld further expand to Windows and Android ecosystems [5, 31]. Work studying agent–computer interfaces shows how UI design and tool affordances affect agent success
(e.g., SWE-agent) [46]. Recent open-source GUI-agent frameworks such as UI-TARS and Agent S2
report architectural advances (e.g., GUI grounding, hierarchical planning) and improved benchmark performance [1, 48]. In light of the documented low end-to-end success rates on complex
multi-step tasks, our framework intentionally constrains the interaction and isolates the choice problem so that we can study rational behavior and the influence of layout and badges without conflating failures from unrelated subroutines (e.g., brittle scrolling or app switching).
Autonomous Shopping Agents and Multimodal Product Understanding.

WebShop frames shop-

ping as instruction-following in a simulated web store, training agents with imitation and reinforcement learning [47]. More recent shopping evaluations include Shopping MMLU (text-based,
multi-task retail skills) and DeepShop (deep research shopping agents with live navigation), as
well as dialog-driven and domain-tuned LLMs for e-commerce such as eCeLLM and LiLiuM
[8, 19, 23, 30, 35]. Vision-language product understanding models (e.g., PUMGPT) address attribute extraction and classification from images and text [45]. Our ACES sandbox complements
these works by providing a controllable setting with randomized listings and visual perturbations to test whether shopping agents exhibit instruction adherence and choice rationality, and to
understand their choice behavior, in the presence of positions, promotions, and badges.
Product Rankings, Platform Design, and Assortment Optimization.

Empirical and theoretical

work shows that positions and rankings can causally shape what consumers examine and buy.
[41] provides experimental evidence on the causal impact of rankings on online search and purchases; [14] quantifies direct and interaction effects between ranking and ratings on consumer
behavior and platform revenue. Our work is related to this line of research as we investigate the
causal impact of rankings (positions) and product attributes on selection by an AI agent. There is
related theoretical and empirical work modelling the consumer’s search process and developing
platforms ranking algorithm [7, 11]. Our work also connects to a long line of work on assortment
optimization which takes customer/AI shopper behavior (as estimated by us) as an input, e.g.,
see [9, 12, 16, 27].
Platform Endorsements and Badges.

Digital platforms deploy badges such as “Best Seller”,

“Overall Pick”, scarcity labels, and strike-through discounts. Recent empirical evidence suggests badges can meaningfully change clicks and add-to-cart probabilities [29]; complementary
work studies badge longevity, uniformity, and bias across marketplaces, and the causal effects of
badges in online communities [3, 22, 28]. Our framework provides, to our knowledge, the first
agent-centric evaluation of whether and how multimodal shopping agents respond to such cues

versus product attributes (price, rating, reviews).
LLMs for simulating human responses and market research.

A rapidly growing stream of liter-

ature leverages LLMs as “silicon samples” to simulate human behavior and preferences. Studies
such as [6, 15, 20, 40] benchmark LLM responses against human survey data to validate these models as low-cost proxies for market research. Similarly, recent work by [42] utilizes data augmentation via LLMs to enhance market analysis. The primary objective of this stream is replication: can
the AI faithfully mimic the human? In contrast, our work views the AI agent not as a proxy for a
human, but as a distinct delegated economic actor. In an agentic economy, the AI is the decisionmaker executing the transaction. We do not ask whether the agent mimics human biases, but
rather what unique biases it introduces. Our framework evaluates the agent’s autonomous choice
function, focusing on how its inherent “priors”—distinct from human preferences—reshape the
demand surface facing sellers and platforms.
Classical recommender systems and industrial

practice highlight personalization’s central role in e-commerce [38]. Recent LLM personalization
efforts create benchmarks and methods for tailoring responses to user-specific preferences [51] and
dataset resources for digital-twin style behavioral modeling [36, 40]. We deliberately use generic
prompts to elicit baseline behavior that is not pre-solved by over-specifying the target item. Our

Algorithmic Delegation.

Theoretical work on delegation studies how a principal can design

mechanisms for delegating to an agent whose incentives or information differ from those of the
principal, and payments are not possible [2]. [25] show that appropriately constrained delegation
can approximate efficient search despite misaligned incentives; [18] find benefits of delegating
search to multiple agents; and [17] study the optimal design of algorithmic delegates to help the
user under information asymmetry, given that certain task categories will be delegated to them by
users while others will not. Our empirical results can be viewed as documenting the outcome of
delegation to AI agents from leading providers in a concrete market setting: given a visual catalog,
what do agents buy and why, and how do platform levers and product attributes translate into
systematic selection patterns?

ACES: Our Sandbox Environment

ACES (Agentic e-CommercE Simulator) consists of: (i) a provider-agnostic browser-use agent that
combines a vision language model (VLM) of choice with tool usage to observe and act on webpages, and (ii) a fully controllable mock e-commerce site (“mock-app”) that renders product listings. The VLM agent is implemented using the LangChain library3 and the Selenium browserdriver4 . The mock-app is a lightweight storefront with search that returns listings from our
pre-specified product universe. For more details, refer to Appendix A.3.

Instruction Following and Basic Rationality Tests

We test two foundational capabilities for reliable AI deployment in e-commerce—instruction following and basic economic rationality—using choice sets constructed to have a single objectively
correct option.

3.1

Experimental Setup

We test for two capabilities which we deem to be foundational for reliable deployment of AI agents
in e-commerce—ability to follow instructions and basic economic rationality. The experiments are
designed such that there is objectively only one product that satisfies the requirements of the test.
Instruction Following Experiments. We evaluate three tasks: (i) choose a product within a
stated budget, (ii) select a specified color, and (iii) select a specified brand. For each task and
selected categories, we minimally edit the default prompt to encode the preference and run 50
trials per task–category with product positions randomly shuffled. See Appendix B.1.1 for details.

4.1

Experiment Setup

We run 1, 000 experiments for each product category in which we randomly permute the position

description actually led to a decrease in market share. This underscores that AI-mediated markets
are volatile and heterogeneous—a change that works for one model may backfire for another.
Consequently, sellers cannot simply “set and forget” AI-generated descriptions. Instead, they require continuous monitoring frameworks—similar to the ACES evaluation framework proposed
in this paper—to iteratively test, validate, and adapt their listings against an evolving landscape
of buying agents.

6.3

Drivers of Improvement

It is natural to ask whether these performance gains are simply due to the AI seller agent producing shorter descriptions. However, our analysis suggests that information reordering and keyword
alignment are the primary drivers. The optimized descriptions are not significantly shorter; rather,
they structurally prioritize high-relevance tokens.
The magnitude of the market share gain appears directly correlated with the extent of this
semantic realignment. In categories where the seller agent executed significant keyword enrichment
or front-loading, the gains were substantial. For instance, in the toilet paper category with GPT5.1, the seller agent enriched a sparse description by adding high-value sensory and functional
keywords (e.g., “2-Ply”, “Septic-Safe”, “Unscented”), resulting in a +15.5 p.p. increase. Similarly,
for staplers, explicitly front-loading the included accessory (“with 1,250 Staples”) alongside the
main product name drove a +9.5 p.p. increase. For example, in the fitness watch category, both
GPT-4.1 (+7.1 p.p.) and Claude Opus 4.5 (+13.0 p.p.) achieved gains not by shortening the text, but
by explicitly appending the term “Fitness”—a keyword absent from the original “GPS Running
Watch” title—to align with the buyer’s search query. For a detailed textual analysis of these drivers
and specific examples across categories, see Appendix B.3.3.
These findings point to a new paradigm for AI-mediated commerce. In traditional e-commerce,
a seller provides a single, static description (e.g., a lamp listed for both “living room” and “office”
use). Our results demonstrate that static descriptions are suboptimal when facing algorithmic buyers with specific queries. In an AI-mediated future, sellers could dynamically tailor descriptions
24

conditional on the incoming query—presenting the same item as an “Ergonomic Office Lamp” to
an agent searching for office gear, and a “Modern Standing Lamp” to an agent designing a living room. We term this query-conditional SEO, where the goal is to dynamically reconfigure the
product’s semantic presentation to maximize alignment with the AI buyer’s specific objective.

7

Extensions: Model Drift and/or Finalization, Headless Interface &
Prompt Variations

Our main results—salient position/rank effects—could in principle be artifacts of (i) temporal
drift in AI models and/or model finalization (ii) the VLM/screenshot interface, or (iii) the model’s
baseline prior induced by generic prompting. We therefore stress-test along three orthogonal axes:
time (re-run experiments a month and a half later), interface (VLM mock-app vs. headless/API
lists), and prompts (default vs. ignore-position vs. prioritize-price).

7.1

Assessing Temporal Model Drift and Impact of Model Finalization

We examine the temporal stability of different models by repeating the same set of choice behavior
experiments with the same default prompt (see Figure 4). We ran the first set of experiments in
early August 2025 and subsequently re-ran the same set of experiments in late September 2025.
We find that Claude Sonnet 4 (see Table 12) and GPT-4.1 (see Table 13) yield very similar estimates
with the position, badge and attribute coefficients moving minimally. We do not observe any
statistically significant drift for these two models over this time interval. For Gemini 2.5 Flash,
signs are directionally stable, but the magnitude change for the top-row coefficient is statistically
significant and increases over time, indicating that the preference of Gemini 2.5 Flash for the top
row increases (see Table 14).
In addition to temporal drift within the same model version, we also analyze the impact of
“model finalization”—specifically, the transition from a preview model to its final release. In Appendix D, we document a natural experiment where Gemini 2.5 Flash Preview was deprecated
and replaced by Gemini 2.5 Flash. We find that this upstream update acted as a significant exogenous demand shock: it inverted position biases (shifting from a negative to a positive top-row
bias) and caused substantial swings in product-level market shares (e.g., flipping the modal product in the office lamp and toilet paper categories), highlighting the volatility sellers face when
underlying buyer models are updated.

7.2

Impact of Headless/API-style interface

While most of our evaluation centers on VLM “computer-use” agents that navigate a retailer’s
website (mock-app), the interaction protocols are already diversifying. Rather than having a website with which an AI agent interacts, platforms may expose a structured product feed via an API,
25

letting agents choose from objects instead of screens. Emerging standards–such as model context
protocol and agent-to-agent protocol–aim to support exactly this type of mode. This raises the
question: are the choice behaviors we document (especially position and badge effects) artifacts
of screenshot parsing and VLM architecture, or do they persist even when the agent sees only
structured data? To probe this, we run a “headless” variant of ACES in which the platform returns
a ranked JSON list of eight products with their attributes (no images), and the agent must respond
with a single JSON choice and a brief reason.
7.2.1

We reuse the data generated to study choice behavior in Section 5—the realized exogeneous randomization of positions, price, ratings, reviews, and tags as prescribed in Table 1, along with
identical text descriptions—so any differences reflect the interface, not the data. We acknowledge
that product images are an essential part of shopping and that production systems will incorporate them even in API flows; our headless setup is therefore a deliberately simplified stress test
of the ranking/metadata channel rather than a substitute for image-aware agents. Similar to the
evaluation of the VLM agents, we begin by considering a generic prompt to evaluate the baseline
or model prior in the headless environment. The default prompt for the headless AI shopping

Ranking matters—and how it matters is model-specific. In the headless setting, all three

by model. For Claude Sonnet 4, the effect is close to monotone, i.e., Position 1 is preferred to Position 2 and so forth. GPT-4.1 shows a Position 1 premium but otherwise a non-monotone profile

Position can lead to drastic changes in the selection rate of the product. For example, for Claude
Sonnet 4, moving a product which is selected 4.1% of the time at position 8 to position 1 leads to
a 7-fold increase in selection rate. The takeaway is twofold: (i) rank order is a first-order driver of
agent choice even without images, and (ii) the induced “attention curve” is heterogeneous across
models. Hence, these position effects are not merely artifacts of VLM screenshot parsing and
are likely to persist under API-style protocols, elevating ranking policy to a core design lever for
agent-mediated commerce.
Sponsored tags depress selection and platform endorsement leads to large lifts. In the
headless/API list, a Sponsored badge reduces choice probabilities even after controlling for rank
and attributes: starting from a 10% baseline, selection falls to 5.4% for Claude Sonnet 4, 1.8% for
GPT-4.1, and 8.9% for Gemini 2.5 Flash. By contrast, a platform Overall Pick endorsement exerts a dominant positive effect: the same baseline jumps to 58.4% for Claude Sonnet 4, 55.6% for
GPT-4.1, and 72.7% for Gemini 2.5 Flash. Interpreted causally (badges are randomized), agents
discount advertising but treat platform endorsement signals as credible. These patterns are not
interface-specific: the mock-app (VLM) experiments show the same directions (see Table 2).
Directional agreement on product attributes. All three models assign negative weight to price
and positive weight to ratings and the number of reviews—directionally consistent with a rational
shopper who prefers cheaper, better-rated, more widely reviewed products. Magnitudes, however, differ sharply across models. A product with a 10% baseline selection probability rises to
22.7% (Claude Sonnet 4), 25.7% (GPT-4.1), and 16.4% (Gemini 2.5 Flash) after a +0.1 rating increase.

UI Setting.

We present in Tables 12, 13 and 14 the parameters of the estimated conditional logit model under
the baseline prompts and the adjusted prompts.
“Ignore Position” prompt does not remove position bias. Explicitly prompting the different
AI agents to be agnostic to the product position has a heterogenous impact on the coefficients
of the position attributes. For Claude Sonnet 4 and GPT-4.1, there is no statistically significant
difference in the position coefficient. Moreover, the other coefficients (badge effects and product
attribute effects) also do not change in a statistically significant manner. For Gemini 2.5 Flash,
we observe that there is an (statistically significant) attenuation in the coefficient of the top-row
and column 2 while the bias for column 3 increases. The key takeaway is that simple prompt
engineering leaves largely unaffected position effects, so eliminating these biases requires design
changes beyond simply prompting.
“Ignore Position and Prioritize price” prompt increases price sensitivity and reduces other
sensitivities. Setting a “ignore position & prioritize price” prompt steepens the price coefficients
sharply (see Tables 12–14). Interpreting these in odds terms, a 10% price decrease raises choice odds

by about 81% for Claude Sonnet 4, 165% for GPT-4.1, and 101% for Gemini 2.5 Flash. As price
becomes central, other attributes downshift: rating weights fall and so do the number of reviews
coefficients; the Overall Pick premium collapses to near zero for Claude Sonnet 4 and GPT-4.1 and
shrinks markedly for Gemini 2.5 Flash.Positional effects compress heterogeneously: for Claude
Sonnet 4, Column 1 flips signwhile for Gemini 2.5 Flash, the top-row coefficient flips positive to
negative. A key takeaway is that light personalization via prompting powerfully re-weights price,
but position effects remain economically meaningful.
7.3.2

Headless Setting.

We now study the impact of change in prompt on the choice behavior in the headless setting.
This is analogous to our exploration of the impact of prompt in the case of VLM agents presented
above. We modify the default prompt (Figure 10) to (i) ignore position (Figure 28) and (ii) ignore
position & prioritize price (Figure 29). Tables 17, 18 and 19 report the pooled (across categories)
estimates across different prompts (as provided in Figures 10, 28 and 29) for Claude Sonnet 4,
GPT-4.1 and Gemini 2.5 Flash respectively. These tables allow us to see how the change in prompt
changes the choice behavior of the AI agents.
Prompting the model to “ignore position” attenuates—but does not eliminate position effects. With the “ignore position” prompt, position effects decline but remain statistically and economically meaningful for the top slots. This echoes the VLM results: explicit instructions reduce,
but do not purge, positional effects.
“Ignore Position & Prioritize price” prompt sharply steepens price sensitivity and reduces
other sensitivities. “Ignore Position & Prioritize price” prompt increases the magnitude of the
price coefficient substantially across all models (Claude Sonnet 4, GPT-4.1 and Gemini 2.5 Flash)—
see Tables 17-19. For intuition, a 10% price increase reduces choice odds by roughly 46%–60%
under the “ignore position and prioritize price” prompt (Claude Sonnet 4 ∼46%, GPT-4.1 ∼59%,
Gemini 2.5 Flash ∼53%), compared to approximately 20% under the default prompt for all the
models. As a side effect of prioritizing price, we observe that the position effects shrink further,
even turning statistically small for GPT-4.1. Across models, the “Overall Pick” tag is very influential under the default prompt. When we set the “ignore position & prioritize price” prompt, this
coefficient falls sharply, indicating that a clear price objective crowds out the platform endorsement.

8

Conclusion and Discussion

Using ACES, we causally measure how frontier AI shopping agents shop. First, position effects are large and model-specific. Second, the Overall Pick tag lifts selection probability (a lot)
while a Sponsored tag depresses it. Third, agents weigh price, ratings, and reviews in the ex29

pected directions, but magnitudes vary widely across models. Fourth, these behaviors persist in a
headless/API setting that exposes ranked JSON lists, under modified prompts, and across model
generations. Fifth, modest seller-side title edits can materially shift selection shares, and upstream
model updates can reallocate demand even when catalogs are unchanged. Together, these results
map a new, AI-mediated demand surface for digital marketplaces.
A natural question is whether these results should be calibrated or compared to a human baseline [for example, as done in 15, in a different context]. While that is an important question, the aim
of this research was to first establish an evaluation framework for agent behavior in e-commerce.
As AI shopping agents increasingly act as de-facto decision makers, agent-centric measurement/evaluation becomes policy-relevant in its own right. That said, several patterns we document
mirror directionally human behavior—ranking/position materially shape choices in digital markets [14, 41], and platform endorsements/badges shift demand [29]. Designs are not identical, so
these are not apples-to-apples comparisons, yet models display human-like regularities (endorsement salience, and sensible weights on price, ratings, and reviews). At the same time, the large
differences across models do imply that most or all models diverge substantially from humans
in their quantitative choice behavior. We expect that in future work, comparing the behavior of
individuals to their digital-twin personas [40] could be be an important complement for specific
policy questions (e.g., market shares).
While the present paper limits itself to a phenomenological (descriptive) investigation, a natural future direction is to further understand why models exhibit the biases they do. One may
wonder how much biases are driven by pre-training (data and/or algorithm), by post-training
(data and/or algorithm), by the vision encoder, or by model drift. Our phenomenological findings suggest that post-training may play a significant role in driving biases (recall the change in
biases from Gemini 2.5 Flash Preview to Gemini 2.5 Flash as seen in Figure 25), that the vision
encoder does not single-handledly drive biases (recall our headless experiments), and that model
drift is not a significant contributor to biases. Our research also suggests important implications
for various stakeholders.
• Platforms. First, our study demonstrates that AI models exhibit strong and heterogeneous
position biases that are not easily mitigated by the interface (web-based vs. headless) or by
alternative prompting. For product ranking, platforms may need to adapt their layout and
ranking systems to account for this. Second, traditional monetization levers for platforms
may be rendered ineffective by AI shoppers. Instead, new levers, such as dynamically optimizing product titles and images on behalf of sellers, could become more powerful tools for
platforms to boost sales. Third, platforms (and regulators) should monitor the impact of AI
assisted shopping on market efficiency and consumer welfare.
• Brands and Sellers. The observed volatility in agent behavior—driven by both inter-provider
30

differences and intra-provider model upgrades—implies that the scope for Generative Engine Optimization (GEO) is substantial and that sellers must continuously adapt product
listings to remain attractive to AI agents.
• Consumers/Buyers. Delegation will reduce search frictions but buyers should understand
their assistant’s unavoidable biases and, where possible, orient their agent by expressing
their preferences (e.g., price sensitivity) via prompting. We believe developing AI agents
that are designed to efficiently elicit buyer preferences is a promising direction for future
work.
• AI shopping-agent developer dashboards. Web-agent leaderboards (e.g., [10]) rank models
on tasks which can be objectively evaluated, but shopping has no single “right” product.
Our results suggest having analogous public dashboards where, instead of ranking models,
developers or evaluators publish ACES-like choice profiles: position biases, sensitivities to
product attributes, positions, tags and endorsements, etc. on standard interfaces (UI and/or
API based). These public dashboards would allow different stakeholders to choose or audit
agents, and compare across models and releases.

A

ACES Framework

We envision an agentic e-commerce workflow in which AI assistants transact on behalf of consumers. These “agents” are simply a VLM (or LLM) given the ability to interact with the external
world through “tools” [1, 34, 39]. These agents can be personalized (for example, through purchase histories) and—instead of users searching and buying themselves—execute the end-to-end
journey: (i) open a browser, (ii) visit a retailer’s site, (iii) issue a query, (iv) parse and navigate
product listings using tool use (scrolling, clicking, screenshotting, etc.), and (v) complete checkout. Figure 12 illustrates this AI shopping agent in operation.

A.1

Business Goal & Context

The ACES framework’s primary purpose to simulate the interaction depicted by Figure 12.
This shopping agent workflow follows a common design pattern that is well-evaluated in the
literature [30, 47], and is already productionized [1, 21].
The shopping agent workflow employed:
1. Opens a browser
2. Navigates to the target platform
3. Searches for a product
4. Captures a screenshot
5. Selects a product using the provided tool
ACES seeks to collapse an agent’s journey to the linear sequence stated above, while accurately
emulating a production-ready shopping agent, interacting with the underlying VLM models in a
uniform manner, and ensuring that the choice behaviors of the model are accessible and measurable. Unlike many real-world examples such as [1, 33] that employ external modules to increase
capabilities in arbitrary tasks, ACES seeks to leave the VLM unassisted to more accurately approximate outcomes.

A.2

High-level Architecture & Approach

Given the complexity of employing VLM agents in the shopping task, ACES is designed with the
following principles:
• Reusability Stakeholders, future researchers, and engineers might look to ACES for future
research. Adapting the mock-app, providing new experiments, and extending the agent has
been kept in mind.
• Provider/Model agnostic Using VLMs in the agent context raises 2 primary hurdles: the
low-level code to interface with these VLMs is not the same; not all VLMs are designed to
interact with external environments as signaled by the diverging implementations of [21].
We solve this by using LangChain7 which provides a single interface for interacting with
VLMs, and collapsing the action space to a single tool call. As a byproduct, the code is
rendered easily extensible.
• Observability Understanding results and debugging must have minimal dependencies on
external frameworks. As such, the entire agent journey is saved to a tree-like file hierarchy
which can be easily inspected.

• Cost & Efficiency To ensure efficiency, the framework includes the boilerplate code to utilize
“batch processing” to allow experimentation to be run at high-throughput.
To conduct experiments which model Figure 12, ACES is comprised of 3 top-level components—
agent, experiments, and sandbox—each responsible for different portions of the workflow, and
who interact through clearly defined interfaces.
The majority of the code is contained within experiments which encapsulates the dataset generation and randomization, collection of the results, and batch processing. The shopping agent
itself is defined within agent; and sandbox contains the “mock-app”.

• Unjustified Sub-optimality. The model recognized the optimal product (e.g., the cheapest or

• Dismissal with justification. The model acknowledged the optimal product but explicitly provided a reason for not selecting it. These reasons often included:

slight deviations.
– Attributing difference to errors. The model sometimes dismissed the optimal option
by attributing the observed difference to a “display error”, a “temporary discount”, an
“outdated listing”, or a “pricing error” on the platform.
– Perceived Insignificance. The model might justify its sub-optimal choice by stating the
price difference was minimal or that there was “no apparent/significant advantage” or
“no functional difference” compared to other options.

We posit that several factors drive this increased market share. The most prominent driver is
the explicit front-loading of the keyword “Office” (e.g., “SUNMORY Office Floor Lamp...”). As
Large Language Models process text sequentially, tokens appearing early in the sequence often
exert a disproportionate influence on the model’s relevance scoring, particularly when matching
against the user’s query intent (“office lamp”). By moving “Office” from the end of the title (where
it was previously truncated or ignored) to the first five words, the seller agent effectively reduced
the semantic friction for the buying agent. Additionally, the recommended descriptions increase
the density of technical specifications (“32W”, “3000LM”, “Stepless Dimmable”) in the title itself.
This likely aligns with the buying agents’ internal heuristics which may associate detailed technical metrics with higher product quality or suitability for a workspace environment.
Furthermore, the behavior of Claude Opus 4.5 highlights the exploitable sensitivities of AI
agents. A shift from 0.0% (essentially invisible to the buyer) to a dominant 41.0% market share
based solely on a title change is a magnitude of volatility rarely observed in human consumer be55

havior. In a traditional market, brand perception and product utility provide a buffer against such
erratic swings. However, the AI buying agent exhibits a “winner-take-all” or “cliff-edge” probability distribution; once the product description crosses a specific threshold of semantic alignment,
the model shifts its preference entirely. This suggests that the algorithmic market is far more brittle
and susceptible to adversarial optimization (or AI-SEO) than human markets, where preferences
are generally more distributed and stable.
We note, however, that the gains in market share are not uniform across different AI buying
agents. For the case of Gemini 3.0 Pro Preview, the market share of SUNMORY only increases
from 0.0% to 7.1% despite the optimized description, whereas for GPT-5.1 the gain is massive.
This demonstrates that there can be non-uniform impact of such interventions depending on the
underlying preferences and architecture of the buying model.

B.3.3

Drivers of Improvement: Textual Analysis of Seller Interventions.

To identify the specific textual drivers of market share improvement, we qualitatively compared
the original product descriptions against the AI-optimized versions across all six buying models.
We observe that successful interventions (which occurred in ∼ 33% of cases) were not random;
they principally employed four distinct semantic strategies: Keyword Front-Loading, Category
Injection, Feature Reordering, and Specification Enrichment.
Strategy 1: Strategic Keyword Front-Loading.

The most consistent driver of performance is

the relocation of specific search query terms to the beginning of the product title, exploiting the
“positional bias” of LLMs.
• Office Lamp (Universal): This was the most effective intervention. Originally, the product
was titled “SUNMORY Floor Lamps for Living Room...”. For the query “office lamp”, this
caused a mismatch in early tokens. The seller agent optimized this by changing the start
to “SUNMORY Office Floor Lamp...” or “SUNMORY LED Office Lamp...”. This simple reordering drove massive gains across the board: GPT-5.1 (+80.4 p.p.), Gemini 2.5 Flash (+52.0
p.p.), Claude Opus 4.5 (+41.0 p.p.), Claude Sonnet 4 (+25.8 p.p.), and even the otherwise
resistant Gemini 3.0 Pro Preview (+7.1 p.p.).
• Stapler (GPT-5.1): The seller agent achieved a +9.5 p.p. gain by moving the accessory count
“with 1,250 Staples” from the middle of the text to immediately follow the product name,
framing it as a complete kit.
Strategy 2: Category Injection and Alignment.

In several cases, the original description was

factually complete but lacked the specific vocabulary used in the buyer’s prompt. The seller agent
successfully “injected” these missing keywords.
• Fitness Watch (Claude Opus 4.5 & GPT-4.1): The focal product (Garmin Forerunner 55) was
originally described as a “GPS Running Watch.” The buyer query, however, was for a “fitness watch.”
– For Claude Opus 4.5 (+13.0 p.p.), the seller agent explicitly added “Fitness Smartwatch” to the title.
– For GPT-4.1 (+7.1 p.p.), the agent appended “Fitness Running Tracker” to the end of
the title.
• Toilet Paper (GPT-5.1): The agent enriched a generic description by adding high-value attributes like “Septic-Safe” and “Fragrance-Free,” driving a +15.5 p.p. increase by matching
potential safety constraints.

59

Strategy 3: Feature Reordering and Heterogeneity.

Even without adding new words, simply

shuffling existing features to the front can yield significant—though heterogeneous—results.
• Toothpaste (GPT-4.1 & Gemini 2.5 Flash): The original title buried the key benefit “Whitening” behind the flavor “Luminous Mint.”
– GPT-4.1 optimized this by swapping the order to “Advanced Whitening Toothpaste,
Luminous Mint,” yielding a +16.6 p.p. gain.
– Gemini 2.5 Flash saw a similar +29.1 p.p. gain from this reordering.
– Interestingly, Claude Sonnet 4 showed no significant improvement from similar changes.
Strategy 4: Specification Enrichment.

For complex appliances, agents appear to reward descrip-

tions that explicitly confirm standard specifications.
• Washing Machine (Gemini 2.5 Flash): The seller agent added specific structural details—“TopLoad” and “Stainless Steel Tub”—that were missing from the original text. This specification enrichment contributed to a significant market share increase (+12.0 p.p.), likely by
increasing the model’s confidence in the product’s quality.

Model Finalization: The case of Gemini 2.5 Flash Preview to Gemini
2.5 Flash

Our initial experiments used Gemini 2.5 Flash Preview, the then-current frontier model by Google
Deepmind. During our testing, we had a natural experiment when Gemini 2.5 Flash Preview was
deprecated and replaced with Gemini 2.5 Flash. We therefore re-ran a subset of experiments with
the “updated” model to measure how an upstream change propagates to AI-mediated demand
and position effects.
Market–share shifts under a model finalization. Figure 24 compares product-level selection
shares when the buyer agent “finalizes” from Gemini 2.5 Flash Preview to Gemini 2.5 Flash, holding the catalog and attributes fixed and only randomizing on-page positions. Shares move substantially, with frequent flips in the modal product: e.g., in office lamp category the mode shifts
from TORCHSTAR (Gemini 2.5 Flash Preview) to SUNMORY (Gemini 2.5 Flash); in toilet paper
from Cottonelle to Angel Soft. Several products experience large swings in share (e.g., Fitbit
Versa: −25.1p.p.; MROCO: −16.5p.p.; Kenmore: −25.6p.p.).

Implications. These results underscore that upstream model updates can function as exogenous demand shocks—relabeling “best sellers” and redistributing shares even when products are
unchanged. For platforms, this volatility affects the stability of ranking and endorsement signals
(e.g., badges, recommendations) and argues for model-aware evaluation pipelines. For sellers,
the findings motivate continuous monitoring and agile listing management: content tuned to yesterday’s model may underperform after an upgrade. More broadly, because model changes alter
both attribute sensitivities and position bias magnitudes, identical designs can yield different outcomes solely due to an upstream revision—reinforcing the need for ongoing auditing, disclosure
of impactful updates, and diversification of strategies across agent ecosystems.
