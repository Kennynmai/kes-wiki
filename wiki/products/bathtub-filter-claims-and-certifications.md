# Bathtub Filter Claims and Certifications

## Why this page exists
Bathtub filter is likely a category where conversion is heavily influenced by health-adjacent, skin-adjacent, and contaminant-reduction claims. That also means claim discipline matters more than in ordinary hardware categories.

## Claim buckets observed in the market
### Contaminant reduction claims
Common observed phrasing includes:
- removes chlorine
- reduces chlorine
- removes chloramine
- removes heavy metals
- removes fluoride
- removes contaminants
- reduces irritants

### Water quality / comfort claims
- softens hard water
- improves odor
- gentler bath water
- smoother skin and hair
- better for sensitive skin

### Outcome / benefit claims
- helps eczema
- helps dry itchy skin
- suitable for baby and kids
- healthier skin and hair
- safer bath water

## Working risk view
### Lower-risk direction
Safer commercial language likely lives closer to:
- designed to reduce chlorine-related odor
- may help improve bath-water feel
- intended for gentler bathing experience
- suitable for consumers seeking lower-chlorine bath routines

### Higher-risk direction
Higher-risk claims likely include:
- removes 99% contaminants (without clear test context)
- removes fluoride (especially if evidence is weak or inconsistent)
- cures or improves eczema
- softens hard water in a technically broad way without defining mechanism
- baby-safe as a safety guarantee without support

## Certification / substantiation questions
This category needs deeper work on what proof standards actually matter in-market.

Key questions:
- Are sellers citing NSF standards directly, or only referencing familiar media like KDF?
- Is certification expected by informed buyers, or is content-led trust enough for most listings?
- Which claims can be made with media-level credibility vs. product-level test proof?
- Does Amazon enforcement meaningfully constrain the strongest health-adjacent claims here?

## Important distinction
A repeated market pattern is likely:
- certified media being used inside a product
versus
- the finished product itself being certified or validated to a standard

Those are not the same. If KES ever enters this category, marketing should avoid blurring that distinction.

## Early working guidance for KES
1. Separate media story from finished-product proof.
2. Avoid disease-relief language unless substantiation and legal review are strong.
3. Prefer “reduce” over “remove” unless proof is specific and product-level.
4. Treat fluoride and hard-water-softening claims as especially sensitive.
5. Build claim hierarchy:
   - core credible claim
   - secondary comfort claim
   - optional segment claim

## Open questions
- Which certification signals appear most often in the strongest-performing listings?
- Are high-performing products actually certified, or just marketed aggressively?
- What claim language seems to correlate with high review trust vs. review disappointment?
- Which claims are likely to trigger complaints, returns, or policy risk?
