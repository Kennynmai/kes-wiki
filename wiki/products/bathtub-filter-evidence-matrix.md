# Bathtub Filter Evidence Matrix

## Why this page exists
This page organizes the bathtub-filter evidence base into a practical lookup matrix.

Use it to answer:
- what kinds of consumer concerns are actually supported by evidence?
- how strong is the support?
- does the evidence support the **problem**, the **segment**, the **content framing**, or the **product claim**?

## Reading guide
### Evidence role meanings
- **problem support**: helps show the concern is real or plausible
- **segment support**: helps show a user segment is credible
- **content support**: helps support careful educational framing
- **claim caution**: helps define what should not be overstated
- **product efficacy**: supports a product-level effectiveness claim

At present, the matrix is strong in **problem support** and **claim caution**, and weak in **product efficacy**.

---

## 1. Chlorine / bathing / skin-barrier concern

### Evidence status
**Moderate problem-level support**

### Key sources
- PubMed: *Free residual chlorine in bathing water reduces the water-holding capacity of the stratum corneum in atopic skin*
- PMC review: *Pooling the evidence: A review of swimming and atopic dermatitis* (full text obtained via Europe PMC)
- National Eczema Society: *Swimming and eczema*
- CDC chloramine irritation context (pool setting)

### What this supports
- chlorine exposure in bathing/swimming-related contexts can be relevant to skin dryness or irritation in some sensitive-skin / AD contexts
- chlorine concern is a plausible buyer motivator
- the better-supported framing is mitigation and balance, not automatic avoidance of swimming

### What this does not support
- direct efficacy of consumer bathtub filters
- blanket statement that all chlorine exposure is universally harmful in eczema

### Best KES use
- problem framing
- sensitive-skin consumer understanding
- claim restraint

## 2. Hard water / eczema / skin comfort concern

### Evidence status
**Moderate-to-strong problem-level support**

### Key sources
- PubMed: *Association between domestic water hardness, chlorine, and atopic dermatitis risk in early life*
- PubMed: *The effect of water hardness on atopic eczema, skin barrier function: A systematic review, meta-analysis*
- PubMed: *Atopic eczema and domestic water hardness*
- UK Biobank adult cohort article

### What this supports
- hard water is a serious category-relevant variable
- hard-water concern is not just marketing language
- infant/child and possibly adult eczema discourse includes real hard-water discussion

### What this does not support
- that a bathtub filter meaningfully softens water in a clinically relevant way
- that water-softening claims by small accessory products are automatically credible

### Best KES use
- category framing
- problem relevance
- caution against loose “hard water softener” claims

---

## 3. Baby / infant segment plausibility

### Evidence status
**Moderate segment-level support**

### Key sources
- early-life / infancy AD risk study involving domestic water hardness
- infant swimming + hard water + atopy interaction study
- parenting-oriented public explainers (secondary, lower-quality support)

### What this supports
- baby/infant bathing is a plausible segment from a consumer-concern perspective
- parents’ sensitivity to water quality is understandable and evidence-adjacent
- the strongest nuanced reading is interaction-based: hard water appears more central than infant swimming alone, but the combination may heighten concern in atopy-prone contexts

### What this does not support
- baby-safe or baby-benefit product claims without product-level validation

### Best KES use
- segment prioritization
- premium storytelling evaluation
- trust-burden awareness

---

## 4. Sensitive skin / eczema segment plausibility

### Evidence status
**Moderate segment-level support**

### Key sources
- chlorine / bathing-water / atopic-skin paper
- water hardness / AD studies
- National Eczema Society guidance

### What this supports
- eczema-prone and sensitive-skin buyers are a credible segment
- the category’s emotional problem framing has evidence-adjacent grounding

### What this does not support
- “improves eczema” product claim
- medicalized marketing language for a general consumer accessory

### Best KES use
- segment mapping
- cautious content strategy
- review/questionnaire design for future validation

---

## 5. Institutional / official educational framing

### Evidence status
**Moderate content-support level**

### Key sources
- EPA CCR information pages
- CDC Healthy Swimming chloramine guidance
- National Eczema Society guidance

### What this supports
- using official references for water-quality awareness and irritation-context education
- balanced consumer education

### What this does not support
- using EPA/CDC/eczema society references as proof of bathtub-filter efficacy

### Best KES use
- educational content planning
- internal editorial guardrails
- anti-overclaim governance

---

## 6. Market-size / category-scale confidence

### Evidence status
**Weak direct support; moderate adjacent-market support**

### Key sources
- shower-filter market reports
- home water filtration market reports
- sparse direct bathtub-filter reporting visibility

### What this supports
- adjacent demand logic exists
- shower filter is a more clearly reportable neighboring category
- bathtub filter may be commercially real but institutionally under-defined

### What this does not support
- direct bathtub-filter TAM
- confident bathtub-filter standalone scale assumptions

### Best KES use
- commercialization caution
- adjacency logic
- prioritizing further demand triangulation

---

## 7. Product efficacy of bathtub filters themselves

### Evidence status
**Weak / insufficient**

### Current state
The current evidence set does **not** strongly support finished-product efficacy claims for bathtub filters as a category.

### Missing evidence
- product-level testing data
- independent comparison of leading products
- strong proof connecting these products to skin outcomes
- robust evidence for broad removal or softening claims under tub-fill conditions

### Best KES use
- red-flag area
- priority gap for future research

---

## 8. Claim safety / overclaim risk

### Evidence status
**Strong caution support**

### Why
Because the evidence layer is nuanced, but marketplace copy is often extreme.

### High-risk claims
- improves eczema
- baby-safe as implied safety guarantee
- removes fluoride / removes broad contaminants without proof
- hard water softener in strong technical sense
- direct disease or therapeutic benefit claims

### Better-supported direction
- gentler-feeling bath water
- chlorine-conscious bathing support
- skin-comfort-oriented educational framing
- cautious, non-medical positioning

---

## Summary view
### Best-supported areas
- hard water as a relevant concern
- chlorine / bathing sensitivity as a plausible concern
- baby / sensitive-skin segment plausibility
- official-context support for educational framing

### Weakest-supported areas
- standalone category scale
- product efficacy
- strong medicalized outcome claims
