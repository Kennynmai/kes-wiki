# Bathtub Filter — Normal-Flow vs Reduced-Flow Evidence Notes (2026-04-13)

## Purpose
Capture source-visible evidence and search-result snippets specifically about how bathtub-filter performance changes under faster versus slower tub-fill flow.

## Method
- Brave web search
- targeted queries for Canopy / Santevia / Sprite
- use of visible snippets from search results plus previously captured Water Filter Guru HTML pages

## Query set
- `Canopy bath filter chlorine slower flow normal flow review GPM`
- `Santevia bath filter fast flow chlorine review GPM`
- `Sprite bath pure bath filter slower flow chlorine review`

## Extracted signals
### Canopy
From search-visible snippet on Water Filter Guru review page:
- slow-flow test average flow rate shown as `0.60 GPM`
- snippet says the filter reduced `100% chlorine` at that slower flow

From search-visible snippet on Water Filter Guru “best bath filters” page:
- scoring references slower faucet flow because that was the rate where the filter performed best
- this implies that performance quality is materially tied to reduced flow conditions

From search-visible snippet on MindBodyGreen launch/review-style page:
- user-facing editorial claim says tub filled in about three minutes and felt comparable to usual flow
- this is useful as a consumer-perception signal, but weaker than measured testing evidence

### Santevia
From search-visible snippet on Water Filter Guru review and comparison page:
- fast-flow test average flow rate shown as `1.65 GPM`
- snippet says chlorine was reduced to `0 PPM` / `100% reduction` at that rate
- this positions Santevia as directionally stronger than products that only work at slower rates

From search-visible snippet on Santevia product page:
- brand claims filtration efficacy up to `99.9% chlorine`
- also uses “optimizes water flow” language

### Sprite
From search-visible snippet on Water Filter Guru review page:
- product can only effectively eliminate chlorine when contact time is impractically long
- snippet says it would take just under `30 minutes` to fill an average `30-gallon` tub under the tested slower-flow condition

From search-visible snippet on Water Filter Guru comparison page:
- slow-flow test average flow rate shown as `0.97 GPM`
- snippet says chlorine fell from `2 PPM` to `0.3 PPM` (`85% reduction`) and still calls this not great because of impractical fill time

### Crystal Quest
From previously captured Water Filter Guru review page text:
- slower-flow result described as `85%` chlorine reduction
- faster-flow result described as `0%` chlorine reduction
- leakage / top-overflow constrained the fastest test condition

From Crystal Quest official product page text:
- recommended flow rate stated as `4–5 GPM`
- this creates a notable tension with editorial test signals that suggest performance weakens materially at higher flow

## Working interpretation
The category appears to split into three broad operating realities:
1. products that may retain credible performance at moderately realistic flow
2. products that work mainly when flow is deliberately slowed down
3. products whose official flow / compatibility language may outrun what review-style testing suggests

## Caveats
- search snippets are directional evidence, not full-source confirmation
- editorial test methodologies may vary and may not perfectly replicate common consumer faucet geometry
- this note should be treated as a scouting input, not a final proof document
