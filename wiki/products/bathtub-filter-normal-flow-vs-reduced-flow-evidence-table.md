# Bathtub Filter Normal-Flow vs Reduced-Flow Evidence Table

## Why this page exists
This page isolates what may be the most important commercial-technical question in the category:
**does the product still appear to work at realistic tub-fill flow, or only when the user slows the water down to an impractical rate?**

## Evidence posture
This is a working evidence table built from:
- captured editorial-review page text
- search-visible snippets tied to identifiable public pages
- official product-page language where useful for contrast

It should be treated as directional, not final.

## Working table
| Brand / route | Faster / more realistic flow signal | Slower / reduced flow signal | Practical implication | Current confidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Santevia bath filter | Water Filter Guru snippet reports ~1.65 GPM with 100% chlorine reduction in a fast-flow test | Same source also reports 100% chlorine reduction at slower flow | Strongest surfaced indication so far that one route may survive more realistic bath-fill conditions | medium |
| Canopy bath filter | Current surfaced evidence is weaker for convincing normal-flow performance; consumer-perception content is more favorable than test-style evidence | Water Filter Guru snippet reports 100% chlorine reduction at ~0.60 GPM slow flow | May be commercially appealing but still exposed to “works only when slowed down” skepticism unless stronger normal-flow proof appears | medium |
| Sprite bath filter | Surfaced evidence suggests normal-flow performance is weak or less convincing | Water Filter Guru snippets suggest slower flow improves results, but one review frames the required contact time as impractical and even then not ideal | High risk of expectation mismatch if marketed too aggressively for realistic tub filling | medium |
| Crystal Quest bath ball | Captured review-page text says 0% chlorine reduction at faster flow in one test condition | Captured review-page text says ~85% chlorine reduction at slower flow | Strong example of a category route where broad claims may outrun realistic fast-fill efficacy | medium |

## What matters strategically
### 1. Flow-rate realism may matter more than claim breadth
A smaller but real benefit at normal filling speed may be commercially stronger than a dramatic claim that only holds under very slow flow.

### 2. The category should not be judged by media stack alone
Seeing KDF / carbon / calcium sulfite sounds reassuring, but the real question is whether the architecture creates enough contact time under normal use.

### 3. Review risk and physics risk are the same risk here
If a user must change behavior substantially to get the promised result, the gap between promise and lived experience becomes a review and return problem.

## Best current KES implication
Any KES route should probably be screened with a hard rule:

> No serious product conviction without evidence at normal or at least commercially realistic bath-fill flow.

This is more important than adding more contaminants to the claim stack.
