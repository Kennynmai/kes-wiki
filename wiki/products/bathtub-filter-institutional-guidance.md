# Bathtub Filter Institutional Guidance

## Why this page exists
This page organizes official and authority-style guidance relevant to bathtub-filter category messaging.

It is **not** a product-efficacy page. It is a guidance page for understanding how official or institution-like sources talk about:
- water quality visibility
- chlorine / chloramine irritation context
- eczema-related bathing nuance

## 1. EPA / local water quality reporting context
### EPA Consumer Confidence Reports (CCR)
Relevant source:
- `https://www.epa.gov/ccr/ccr-information-consumers`

What this gives KES:
- an official path for consumers to check annual local water quality reporting
- a more credible educational angle than generalized “tap water is bad” copy

Potential KES use:
- educational content about checking local water reports
- market-education framing around water awareness

Important limit:
- EPA CCR context is about public drinking water quality reporting, not bathtub-filter product validation

## 2. CDC / chloramine irritation context
### CDC Healthy Swimming — chloramines and pool operation
Relevant source:
- `https://www.cdc.gov/healthy-swimming/toolkit/chloramines-and-pool-operation.html`

What it says at a high level:
- chloramines can irritate skin, eyes, and respiratory tract in pool settings

Why it matters:
- useful for contextualizing irritation language around chlorinated-water exposure

Important limit:
- pool-maintenance context is not the same as household bathtub conditions
- should be used for nuance, not stretched into direct bathtub product claims

## 3. Eczema-specific institutional nuance
### National Eczema Society — swimming and eczema
Relevant source:
- `https://eczema.org/information-and-advice/triggers-for-eczema/swimming-and-eczema/`

Why this source matters a lot:
- it acknowledges that chlorine may contribute to dryness / irritation
- it also acknowledges that chlorine is not simply one-dimensionally harmful in all eczema contexts
- this makes it a valuable source for avoiding simplistic anti-chlorine marketing narratives

Potential KES use:
- internal claim-discipline reference
- balanced educational content
- guardrail against exaggerated “eczema-relief” promise structures

## 4. What institutional guidance does and does not give us
### It helps with
- consumer education framing
- nuance around water concerns
- trust-building content structure
- avoiding overstatement

### It does not give us
- finished bathtub-filter efficacy proof
- certification proof for any product
- direct support for strong medical-style claims

## 5. Working guidance rules for KES
### Safe use of institutional context
Good uses:
- “Consumers can review local water-quality reporting”
- “Some people with sensitive skin are attentive to chlorine / water-hardness issues”
- “Skin response to chlorinated water can be nuanced”

### Unsafe use of institutional context
Avoid converting official context into product proof, e.g.:
- “CDC/EPA proves our bathtub filter improves eczema”
- “official guidance confirms our filter removes what matters most”
- “institutional sources prove bathtub filters are necessary”

## 6. Editorial stance for future KES content
If KES ever publishes external-facing educational content in this area, the tone should likely be:
- informed
- balanced
- non-alarmist
- specific about uncertainty
- careful about the difference between local water awareness and product efficacy
