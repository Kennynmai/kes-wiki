## Compliance / standards signals
### NSF/ANSI 177
Visible public guidance strongly indicates:
- shower filters certified to NSF/ANSI 177 are certified to reduce **free available chlorine**
- the standard is shower-filtration-specific and aesthetics-oriented

### Strategic compliance implication
This creates a major claim-discipline issue for the bathtub-filter category:
- brands may borrow credibility from shower-filter standards
- but the standard itself does not automatically support:
  - hard-water-softening claims
  - broad heavy-metal removal claims
  - skin/eczema outcome claims
  - bath-specific performance claims unless actually validated for that setup

### Wording-risk pattern already visible
Public pages repeatedly mix together language such as:
- certified to
- tested to
- independently tested
- clinically tested
- clinically proven
- pediatrician recommended

This wording mix is important because it can blur very different evidence levels into one impression of certainty.
