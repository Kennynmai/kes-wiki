# Raw Capture — Bathtub Filter Academic and Institutional Evidence Deepening (2026-04-11)

## Purpose
Second-pass evidence capture focused on strengthening the academic / institutional layer behind bathtub-filter category logic.

## A. Academic evidence directly relevant to water / bathing / eczema logic
### 1. Residual chlorine in bathing water and atopic skin
Source observed:
- PubMed: *Free residual chlorine in bathing water reduces the water-holding capacity of the stratum corneum in atopic skin*
- URL: `https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12692355/`

Visible summary signal:
- residual chlorine in bathing water may reduce water-holding capacity of the stratum corneum in atopic skin
- article context explicitly mentions dry skin / exacerbated cutaneous inflammation after bathing or frequent pool exposure in some AD patients

Why this matters:
- strong problem-level evidence for why chlorine in bath-related water can matter in sensitive-skin contexts

Important limit:
- still not evidence that consumer bathtub filters reliably solve the issue

### 2. Domestic water hardness, chlorine, and AD risk in early life
Source observed:
- PubMed: *Association between domestic water hardness, chlorine, and atopic dermatitis risk in early life: A population-based cross-sectional study*
- URL: `https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27241890/`

Visible summary signal:
- high domestic water CaCO3 levels associated with increased risk of AD in infancy
- influence of increased total chlorine levels remains uncertain
- snippet notes that an intervention trial would be needed to determine whether a device reducing CaCO3 could reduce risk

Why this matters:
- this is especially useful because it separates two ideas:
  1. water hardness appears more clearly implicated
  2. chlorine signal is less settled
- also useful because it directly cautions against jumping from association to product claim

### 3. Systematic review / meta-analysis on water hardness and atopic eczema
Source observed:
- PubMed: *The effect of water hardness on atopic eczema, skin barrier function: A systematic review, meta-analysis*
- URL: `https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33259122/`

Visible summary signal:
- positive association between hard-water areas and atopic eczema in children
- no evidence that domestic water softeners improve objective disease severity in established atopic eczema
- there may be a role of water hardness in initiation of disease

Why this matters:
- very important for KES claim discipline
- it supports category relevance of hard water as a consumer concern
- but it weakens any simplistic “soften water and eczema improves” product story

### 4. Earlier evidence linking hard water and eczema in children
Source observed:
- PubMed: *Atopic eczema and domestic water hardness*
- URL: `https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9716057/`

Visible summary signal:
- exposure to hard water in the home may increase risk of eczema in school-age children

Why this matters:
- shows this is not a brand-new hypothesis; it has longstanding epidemiologic discussion behind it

### 5. Hard water + infant swimming + atopy interaction
Source observed:
- PubMed: *Interactions between domestic water hardness, infant swimming and atopy in the development of childhood eczema*
- URL: `https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22591883/`

Visible summary signal:
- exposure to hard water and infant swimming interact with atopic status to increase childhood eczema prevalence
- snippet references barrier breaching by detergents or salts in hard water and chlorine-based oxidants

Why this matters:
- useful for understanding why the market repeatedly converges on baby / child bathing + water-quality anxiety

## B. Institutional / authority-style references relevant to consumer education
### 1. EPA: Consumer Confidence Reports (CCR)
Source observed:
- EPA CCR Information for Consumers
- URL: `https://www.epa.gov/ccr/ccr-information-consumers`

Visible summary signal:
- EPA explains drinking water quality reports and how consumers can access local annual water quality reports

Why this matters:
- important official source for local water-quality context
- useful in content strategy because consumers can be educated to check local water reports rather than rely only on generalized fear messaging

Practical implication for KES:
- future content could responsibly reference local water-quality awareness without overclaiming product performance

### 2. CDC: chloramines and irritation context
Source observed:
- CDC Healthy Swimming: Chloramines and Pool Operation
- URL: `https://www.cdc.gov/healthy-swimming/toolkit/chloramines-and-pool-operation.html`

Visible summary signal:
- chloramines can irritate skin, eyes, and respiratory tract in pool contexts

Why this matters:
- useful official support for irritation language in chlorinated-water contexts
- but context is pool operation, not household bathwater

Important limit:
- should not be overextended to claim equivalent household bathtub effects without caution

### 3. National Eczema Society
Source observed:
- *Swimming and eczema*
- URL: `https://eczema.org/information-and-advice/triggers-for-eczema/swimming-and-eczema/`

Visible summary signal:
- acknowledges chlorine can cause dryness
- also acknowledges chlorine / diluted bleach is not straightforwardly “all bad” for eczema

Why this matters:
- one of the best authority-style caution sources against simplistic anti-chlorine marketing
- reinforces the need for balanced, non-hysterical product framing

## C. Evidence-layer interpretation for category strategy
### What this deeper pass strengthens
- there is credible underlying problem logic around water hardness, chlorine exposure, bathing, and skin barrier concerns
- baby / infancy / eczema-related consumer concern is not invented out of thin air
- hard water may be a more robust problem narrative than chlorine alone in some evidence streams

### What this deeper pass also strengthens
- product claims must stay disciplined
- association-level or mechanism-level evidence should not be converted into disease-outcome or finished-product efficacy claims
- “helps eczema” remains especially risky without product-level evidence

## D. Strong working evidence rule for KES
Use the evidence layer to support:
- why the category matters
- why consumers care
- why certain segments exist

Do NOT use the evidence layer alone to support:
- direct efficacy claims for a specific bathtub filter
- broad contaminant-removal claims
- hard-water-softening disease-benefit claims
