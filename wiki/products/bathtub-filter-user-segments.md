# Bathtub Filter User Segments

## Why this page exists
Bathtub filter does not appear to be a generic mass-market utility purchase. It looks more like a problem-led category where positioning will depend on which user segment is prioritized.

## Working segment map
### 1. Parents buying for babies / young children
Observed signals:
- baby-safe language
- baby bath filter naming
- gentler water positioning

Why this segment matters:
- strong emotional motivation
- easier premium storytelling
- willingness to pay may be higher if trust is established

Risks:
- highest trust burden
- stronger sensitivity around safety claims

### 2. Sensitive-skin / eczema / dry-skin users
Observed signals:
- eczema mention appears repeatedly
- dry itchy skin language is common
- “irritants” is a recurring bridge word

Why this segment matters:
- pain is strong and recurring
- before/after storytelling may be persuasive

Risks:
- claim language can become medical or quasi-medical too quickly
- perceived performance gap can trigger negative reviews

### 3. Chlorine / odor / water-feel improvers
Observed signals:
- removes chlorine
- reduces odor
- gentler water feel

Why this segment matters:
- cleaner and more defensible positioning
- less medically sensitive than eczema claims

Risks:
- may feel less urgent than baby/sensitive-skin positioning
- may convert poorly if benefit is not noticeable

### 4. Hard-water comfort seekers
Observed signals:
- hard water softener language is common in listings
- skin/hair/nails benefits are often tied to this message

Why this segment matters:
- large adjacent shower-filter style demand logic
- easier to connect with hair/skin outcomes

Risks:
- many low-cost products may not truly “soften” water in the strongest technical sense
- overclaim risk is high

### 5. Wellness / premium bath users
Observed signals:
- smoother skin
- spa-like bath quality
- comfort / premium self-care framing

Why this segment matters:
- aligns with premium aesthetic product design opportunities
- may reduce dependence on hard scientific claims if framed carefully

Risks:
- may be too soft as a primary wedge if problem intensity is low

## Current view
The most commercially interesting likely wedges are:
1. baby / child bathing
2. sensitive skin / irritant-conscious households
3. chlorine-conscious comfort seekers

The cleanest entry point from a claims-risk perspective may be the third one, even if the first two appear more emotionally powerful.

## Positioning question for KES
If KES pursues this category, it will need to choose whether the product is primarily:
- a baby-care adjacent bath accessory
- a sensitive-skin comfort product
- a water-quality comfort accessory
- a premium bathing upgrade

Trying to be all four at once may create confused positioning and risky copy.

## Open questions
- Which segment shows the clearest willingness to pay?
- Which segment has the most credible repeat-purchase behavior?
- Which segment offers the best balance of demand strength and claim safety?
- Which segment best matches KES brand stretch?
