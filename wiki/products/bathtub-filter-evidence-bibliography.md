# Bathtub Filter Evidence Bibliography

## Why this page exists
This page indexes the evidence base behind bathtub-filter category logic.

Its purpose is to separate:
1. **problem-level evidence** — why consumers care about chlorine, hard water, bathing, and skin comfort
2. **product-level evidence** — whether bathtub filters themselves are proven effective

At present, the evidence base is much stronger for **problem relevance** than for **finished-product efficacy**.

## Evidence-use rule
Use this page to support:
- category relevance
- consumer problem framing
- segment plausibility
- claim-discipline and caution

Do **not** use this page alone to support:
- finished-product efficacy claims
- disease-improvement claims
- strong hard-water-softening promises for bathtub filters

## A. Academic / peer-reviewed evidence

### 1. Free residual chlorine in bathing water reduces the water-holding capacity of the stratum corneum in atopic skin
- Source type: PubMed-indexed article
- URL: `https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12692355/`
- Access level obtained in this session: **long abstract extracted from page HTML**
- What it supports:
  - chlorine in bathing-water contexts can be relevant to skin-barrier concerns in atopic skin
- What it does **not** support:
  - that a given bathtub filter product improves eczema or skin outcomes
- Evidence role: **problem-level support**

### 2. Association between domestic water hardness, chlorine, and atopic dermatitis risk in early life
- Source type: PubMed-indexed population-based cross-sectional study
- URL: `https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27241890/`
- Access level obtained in this session: **long abstract extracted from page HTML**
- Visible abstract signal:
  - higher domestic water CaCO3 associated with increased AD risk in infancy
  - chlorine influence remained uncertain in the visible summary
- What it supports:
  - hard water is a serious category-relevant variable
- What it does **not** support:
  - direct bathtub-filter efficacy
- Evidence role: **problem-level support**

### 3. The effect of water hardness on atopic eczema, skin barrier function: A systematic review, meta-analysis
- Source type: PubMed-indexed systematic review / meta-analysis
- URL: `https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33259122/`
- Visible abstract signal:
  - hard water associated with AE in children
  - no evidence that domestic water softeners improve objective severity in established AE
- What it supports:
  - hard water matters in category logic
  - claim discipline is necessary
- What it does **not** support:
  - “soften water and eczema improves” product storytelling
- Evidence role: **high-value caution source**

### 4. Atopic eczema and domestic water hardness
- Source type: PubMed-indexed article
- URL: `https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9716057/`
- What it supports:
  - hard water / eczema linkage has longstanding research history
- Evidence role: **background support**

### 5. Interactions between domestic water hardness, infant swimming and atopy in the development of childhood eczema
- Source type: PubMed-indexed article
- URL: `https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22591883/`
- What it supports:
  - interaction between hard water, infant swimming, and atopic status in childhood eczema discussion
- Evidence role: **baby / infant segment support**

### 6. The association between domestic hard water and eczema in adults from the UK Biobank cohort study
- Source type: PMC / cohort study
- URL: `https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9804584/`
- What it supports:
  - hard-water / eczema discussion extends beyond infant-only context
- Evidence role: **adult-context support**

## Evidence strength summary
### Strongest support currently available
- hard water as a real eczema-related problem variable
- chlorine / bathing-water sensitivity as a plausible skin-barrier concern
- bathtub filter category logic as a credible consumer concern area

### Weakest / missing areas
- product-level finished bathtub-filter efficacy evidence
- direct trial-level support for bathtub filters improving consumer skin outcomes
- robust standalone bathtub-filter market-size evidence
