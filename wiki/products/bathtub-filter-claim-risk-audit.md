# Bathtub Filter Claim Risk Audit

## Version links
- Newer working page: [[bathtub-filter-claim-risk-audit-v2]]

## Table
| Claim category | Typical wording seen in market | Evidence base status | Risk level | KES view |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Chlorine reduction | reduces chlorine / chlorine smell | relatively strongest and most believable | medium | usable if scoped tightly |
| Free available chlorine under standard-like conditions | certified/tested to reduce free chlorine | strongest if truly certified/tested and conditions are clear | low-medium | best technical trust signal |
| Broad contaminant removal | removes heavy metals, VOCs, pesticides, PFAS, etc. | often broad and route-unspecific in surfaced content | high | avoid unless validated narrowly |
| Hard-water softening | softens hard water / balances hard water | weak support for compact bathtub-filter claims | high | high caution / likely avoid |
| Sensitive-skin comfort | gentler bath, softer-feeling water | commercially useful, evidence-adjacent but not product-proof | medium | usable with restraint |
| Baby-safe / family-safe | safer bath time for babies | emotionally powerful but proof burden rises fast | high | high caution |
| Eczema improvement | helps eczema / flare-ups / improves skin hydration | strongest marketing temptation, weakest clean substantiation for finished product | very high | avoid direct efficacy claims |
| Clinically tested/proven | clinically tested / clinically proven | requires aggressive verification; often surfaced without enough visible context | very high | audit aggressively before trusting |

## Working rule
The farther a claim moves from “free chlorine reduction” toward “skin outcome improvement,” the more likely it is to outrun the current defensible evidence base.
