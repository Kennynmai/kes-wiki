# Raw Capture — Bathtub Filter Academic and Institutional Evidence (2026-04-11)

## Purpose
Capture academic, clinical-adjacent, and institutional evidence relevant to bathtub-filter category logic. This is not proof that bathtub filters themselves work; it is evidence around the underlying problem statements buyers care about.

## Academic / clinical-adjacent signals observed
### Chlorine and atopic / sensitive skin
#### PubMed: free residual chlorine in bathing water and atopic skin
Source observed:
- PubMed: “Free residual chlorine in bathing water reduces the water-holding capacity of the stratum corneum in atopic skin”

Visible takeaway from snippet:
- residual chlorine in bathing water may reduce water-holding capacity of the stratum corneum in atopic skin

Category implication:
- supports why chlorine-sensitive bathing could be a plausible concern for eczema / sensitive-skin households
- does not prove that a bathtub filter device solves the problem

#### Review / evidence pooling on swimming and atopic dermatitis
Source observed:
- PMC review on swimming and atopic dermatitis

Visible takeaway from snippet:
- chlorine exposure can be irritating in some contexts
- review references prior evidence that residual chlorine in bathing water may reduce water-retaining properties of the stratum corneum in AD

Category implication:
- helps justify why chlorine/skin-barrier language appears in the market
- still not product-level proof for bathtub filters

### Hard water and eczema associations
#### UK Biobank / related epidemiologic evidence
Sources observed:
- PMC article on domestic hard water and eczema in adults
- JACI / ScienceDirect references on domestic water hardness, chlorine, and atopic dermatitis risk in early life

Visible takeaway from snippets:
- high domestic water hardness has been associated with eczema risk in children and studied in adults
- infant exposure to above-average water hardness was associated with increased eczema risk in one cited study context

Category implication:
- helps explain why “hard water” appears as a recurring buyer and seller theme
- still does not mean a low-cost bathtub filter truly softens water in a meaningful technical sense

### Important complexity signal: chlorine is not simply “bad” in all dermatology contexts
Observed sources:
- PubMed / PMC material on bleach baths for atopic dermatitis
- National Eczema Society guidance on swimming and eczema

Visible takeaway from snippets:
- diluted bleach baths can be used therapeutically in some AD contexts
- chlorine can cause dryness / irritation in some situations, but antiseptic effects may also be clinically relevant in others

Category implication:
- this is an important caution against simplistic marketing narratives like “all chlorine is harmful for eczema”
- product messaging should not overstate the dermatologic logic

## Institutional / official-style signals observed
### National Eczema Society
Observed signal:
- guidance around swimming and eczema, acknowledging chlorine, dryness, and practical mitigation behavior

Category implication:
- useful for understanding real-world skin-management advice language
- more relevant as consumer-cognition context than as bathtub-filter proof

### Parents / pediatric / family-oriented explainer coverage
Observed signal:
- mainstream parenting explainers frame bath filters as possibly useful for sensitive skin or eczema-prone households

Category implication:
- supports the existence of mainstream consumer curiosity
- not strong technical proof

## Working evidence interpretation
### What is supported at a category-problem level
- chlorine exposure in water is plausibly relevant to skin comfort / dryness / irritation conversations
- hard water has real association-level relevance in eczema discourse
- baby/sensitive-skin positioning is not purely invented; it maps to broader skin-barrier concerns in public and academic discourse

### What is NOT supported yet
- that bathtub filters as currently sold reliably create clinically meaningful outcomes
- that broad contaminant-removal claims are substantiated
- that “hard water softening” claims from small accessory filters are technically robust
- that baby/eczema claims are safe to make without strong product-level evidence

## Key caution note for KES
The evidence base helps justify why consumers care. It does NOT automatically justify strong product claims.

That distinction should be preserved clearly in any future KES exploration.
