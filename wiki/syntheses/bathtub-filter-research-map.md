# Bathtub Filter Research Map

## Framing
This page is the reusable research map for the bathtub filter topic. Its purpose is to structure future capture, source reading, and write-back so the category can be explored systematically instead of through scattered notes.

## Findings
### What needs to be established first
1. demand existence
2. user pain segmentation
3. product-form reality
4. claims / compliance constraints
5. platform-commercial fit

### Likely research tracks
#### 1. Search-intent track
Questions:
- Do users search “bathtub filter” directly?
- What adjacent terms dominate instead?
- Is the demand framed around babies, skin, chlorine, hard water, eczema, wellness, or odor?

Deliverables:
- keyword map
- intent clusters
- naming recommendations

#### 2. Product-form track
Questions:
- What forms actually exist in-market?
- Are many “filters” actually treatment accessories or media products rather than true filtration devices?
- Which form factors are easiest to explain and support operationally?

Deliverables:
- form-factor taxonomy
- install/compatibility matrix
- complexity/risk notes

#### 3. Consumer-pain track
Questions:
- Which customer problems are repeated most often in reviews?
- Which pains appear emotional vs. functional?
- Which customer segments are most active?

Likely segment buckets:
- baby / children
- sensitive skin / eczema
- hard-water households
- chlorine-smell / comfort seekers
- wellness / premium bath users

#### 4. Claims / trust track
Questions:
- What claims are top brands using?
- Which claims appear risky or unverifiable?
- What certifications or test standards are being signaled?

Deliverables:
- claims library
- red-flag claims list
- certification gap notes

#### 5. Commercial track
Questions:
- What price bands exist?
- Is there replacement-cartridge economics?
- Is repeat purchase meaningful?
- What are the typical low-rating reasons?

Deliverables:
- competitor price map
- margin hypothesis notes
- failure-mode / return-risk map

## Fact vs inference
### Facts currently established
- KES is evaluating category adjacency beyond current core lines.
- Bathtub filter is now a tracked KES research topic.

### Current inferences only
- The category may be education-heavy.
- The category may carry higher claims/compliance risk than ordinary hardware.
- The category may have stronger premium/problem-solution storytelling than commodity hardware.

These require real market evidence before stronger conclusions are written back to official surfaces.

## Tradeoffs / unknowns
- attractive problem-solution storytelling vs. higher trust burden
- premium positioning potential vs. certification/claims constraints
- accessory simplicity vs. compatibility complexity
- repeat-filter economics vs. support and complaint load

## Implications
If this category proves real, it could open a broader KES research branch around:
- home water quality accessories
- skin-sensitive bathroom products
- premium bathing accessories
- filtration-adjacent bathroom hardware

If weak, it may still be useful as a demand-sensing case study for how to evaluate health- or wellness-adjacent bathroom categories.
